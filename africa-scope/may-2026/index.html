From proverb to practice

"When the roots of the tree are deep, the wind cannot uproot it."

— Malian proverb

Across Africa this month, the strongest stories are not about speed. They are about foundations. From digital sovereignty and AI infrastructure to healthcare systems, fintech expansion and cultural preservation, organisations across the continent are investing in long-term resilience rather than short-term visibility.

For communicators, the lesson is clear. Reputation is no longer built through messaging alone. It is built through credibility, consistency and proof.

  Healthcare
DRC · Public Health

WHO raises Ebola risk to highest level in DRC

The World Health Organisation has declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern following a highly aggressive outbreak of the rare Bundibugyo Ebola virus strain. The variant does not respond to existing standard vaccines.

Health workers face uphill containment challenges across dense urban zones. Global emergency medical teams are rushing specialised quarantine resources to the border regions.

Why this matters
More than a public health emergency. It is a test of institutional trust, crisis communication and state resilience in one of Africa's most fragile regions. In conflict-affected environments, misinformation, displacement and weak healthcare access can spread almost as quickly as the virus itself.

Spotlight  ·  Culture

Why Burundian folklore belongs in Africa's future-of-learning conversation.

Reported by ACG editorial. Bujumbura, May 2026.

A parent reads a Kirundi-language children's story in Bujumbura. Photo courtesy of the Harabaye series.

When Burundian author Aïta Chancella Kanyange struggled to find books that reflected the realities of her children's lives, she decided to create them herself.

Her trilingual children's book series, Harabaye: Once Upon a Time, brings traditional Burundian folklore to life in Kirundi, French and English, giving young readers stories rooted in their own culture, language and lived experience.

What began as personal frustration is now part of a broader movement around culturally relevant education, indigenous storytelling and language preservation across Africa. Parents in Bujumbura increasingly turn to the books and live reading sessions as alternatives to imported narratives.

Why this matters
For communicators and brands, the lesson is powerful. Localisation is not translation. It is identity, trust and relevance. The organisations that resonate most deeply may be those that understand culture not as decoration, but as infrastructure.
